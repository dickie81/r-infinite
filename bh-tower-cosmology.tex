To extract a finite value, we employ zeta-function regularization via the heat kernel.

The heat-kernel trace associated to \(N\) is
\[

\]
This expression diverges as \(t \to 0^+\) (ultraviolet) and encodes the full spectral information.

To isolate the contribution from excited states (standard infrared subtraction of the true vacuum, exactly as in Casimir calculations and bosonic string theory), we define the regularized kernel
\[
K_>(t) = K(t) - 1 = \frac{e^{-t}}{1 - e^{-t}} = \sum_{k=1}^\infty e^{-t k}.
\]

The associated spectral zeta function is obtained through the Mellin transform:
\[

\]
Substituting the geometric series for \(K_>(t)\) and interchanging sum and integral (justified by absolute convergence for \(\operatorname{Re}(s) > 1\)) immediately yields
\[

\]
which is the Riemann zeta function. The integral representation above supplies the unique meromorphic continuation of \(\zeta(s)\) to the entire complex plane, with a single simple pole at \(s=1\) and holomorphic everywhere else.

The physically relevant value at the negative integer \(s = -1\) follows by analytic continuation. One standard route is the functional equation
\[

\]
Substituting \(s = -1\) and using \(\zeta(2) = \pi^2/6\), \(\Gamma(2) = 1\), and \(\sin(-\pi/2) = -1\) gives
\[
\zeta(-1) = -\frac{1}{12}.
\]
Equivalently, the Bernoulli-number formula for negative integers \(\zeta(1-2m) = (-1)^m B_{2m}/(2m)\) with \(m=1\) and \(B_2 = 1/6\) yields the same result. This is not arbitrary: it is the unique finite part that survives after subtracting the divergent pole terms in any exponential cutoff scheme. For example, with a soft cutoff \(\sum_{k=1}^\Lambda k e^{-\epsilon k}\) one obtains
\[
