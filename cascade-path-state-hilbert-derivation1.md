# Derivation 1: cascade-native path-state Hilbert space

This document attempts the central open question of Option 2:
**how does the cascade's slicing recurrence interact with a
path-state interpretation, and which of {constrained, free, gauged}
does the cascade's structure select?**

The result is **partial success in the gauged reading**: the cascade
naturally selects gauged path-states, with $U(1)$ generated by the
complex structure $J$ as the base gauge group. This closes the Hopf
gap automatically. Variable Hilbert dimension comes from gauge
representations at Adams gauge layers, not directly from path
length.

## The three readings, made precise

For a path $\gamma$ descending through cascade layers
$d \in [d_a, d_b]$, the cascade's discrete propagator
(Part~II Theorem~`discrete-schrodinger`, line 720–731) gives
$\tilde\psi_{d-1} = i\,\tilde\psi_d$ on lapse-normalised states.

### Reading C (constrained)

Layer-states are *determined* by the topmost-layer state via the
cascade evolution:
$$\tilde\psi_d = i^{d_b - d}\,\tilde\psi_{d_b} \quad \forall d \in \gamma.$$

**Implication**: the path-state is a single object — the topmost
state — with all lower layers being its $i$-rotated images.
**Hilbert dim is independent of path length** and equals the
single-layer dim (e.g., $\mathbb{C}^2$ for the observer's $\mathbb{R}^4$).

### Reading F (free)

Layer-states $\tilde\psi_d$ at each $d \in \gamma$ are *independent*:
the path-state space is $\bigoplus_{d \in \gamma} S^{d-1}$.
**Hilbert dim grows with path length.**

But this discards the cascade's slicing recurrence — the relation
$\tilde\psi_{d-1} = i\,\tilde\psi_d$ becomes vestigial. Reading F
adds structure not present in the cascade.

### Reading G (gauged)

Layer-states are related by a gauge transformation $g(d)$ at each
layer:
$$\tilde\psi_{d-1} = g(d)\,\tilde\psi_d, \quad g(d) \in G_d,$$
where $G_d$ is the **allowed gauge group at layer $d$**.

The cascade's specific choice $g(d) = i$ is a particular gauge; the
gauge group consists of *all* $g(d)$'s the cascade allows.

**Physical states** = path-states modulo gauge transformations.

**Hilbert dim** = depends on $G_d$ and its representation along the
path; NOT directly on path length.

## Which reading does the cascade select?

Three pieces of cascade-internal evidence point to Reading G:

### Evidence 1: the cascade's complex structure $J$ generates a
continuous $U(1)$

Part~II Theorem~`complex` (line 555–574) derives $J$ as the
cascade's complex structure on $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$, with $J^2 = -I$.
This $J$ generates a continuous $U(1) \subset SO(2n)$:
$$R_\phi = \cos\phi\,I + \sin\phi\,J.$$

The cascade discrete propagator $L(d) = i\,N(d) = J\,N(d)$ commutes
with $R_\phi$ (verified directly in
`cascade-hopf-bloch-finding.md`):
$$[L(d),\,R_\phi] = 0 \quad \forall \phi.$$

So the cascade evolution is *invariant* under the continuous $U(1)$
generated by $J$. Two layer-states differing by an $R_\phi$
transformation evolve in lockstep under cascade descent. Under
Reading G, this $U(1)$ is *the* gauge group at every cascade layer.

The cascade's specific evolution $g(d) = i$ is the choice
$\phi = \pi/2$. Other $\phi$'s are dynamically equivalent. Reading
G makes this equivalence the structural fact.

### Evidence 2: Adams' theorem provides the layer-dependent gauge enhancement

At gauge layers (Part~IVa~§2):
- $d=12$: $\rho(12) - 1 = 3$, gauge $G_{12} = SU(3)$.
- $d=13$: $\rho(13) - 1 = 1$, gauge $G_{13} = SU(2)$.
- $d=14$: $\rho(14) - 1 = 0$, gauge $G_{14} = U(1)$.

Adams' theorem identifies the *maximum* number of linearly
independent vector fields on $S^{d-1}$. Each linearly independent
vector field contributes to a continuous symmetry of the sphere. So
$\rho(d)-1$ is the rank of the largest continuous group acting on
$S^{d-1}$ by isometries (modulo the universal $J$-rotation).

Under Reading G, the gauge group at layer $d$ is:
- $G_d = U(1)$ (from $J$) at non-gauge layers.
- $G_d = U(1) \times SU(2)$ at $d=13$, etc. — but specifically the
  cascade's Part IVa identifies this as just the gauge layer's
  group, not a product.

Reading G accommodates Adams: the gauge group at each layer is the
maximum cascade-allowed transformation, with $U(1)$ as the base
and Adams enhancements at distinguished layers.

### Evidence 3: Reading C is too restrictive; Reading F is too permissive

Reading C gives a single-layer Hilbert dim, missing the obvious
fact that the cascade has *more* structure than a single sphere
(Bott periodicity, distinguished layers, gauge windows). Reading
C reduces the cascade to its observer-layer state space, throwing
away everything above.

Reading F gives a layer-product Hilbert space, but discards the
slicing recurrence. The cascade's defining relation
$V_{d+1}/V_d = \sqrt{\pi}R(d+1)$ becomes irrelevant to the
quantum state space, which is unfaithful to the cascade's actual
structure.

Reading G threads between these: layer-states are *related* by
cascade evolution (not free), but the relation has *gauge
freedom* (not single-state-determined), and the gauge structure
varies layer-by-layer (not uniform).

## What Reading G delivers

### Hopf gap closure

At every layer, the gauge group includes the continuous $U(1)$
generated by $J$. Layer-states $\tilde\psi$ and $R_\phi\,\tilde\psi$
are gauge-equivalent. The physical state space at the observer
($d=4$) is therefore $S^3 / U(1) = \mathbb{C}P^1 = S^2$, recovered
via the Hopf bundle $S^3 \to S^2$.

**This is the cascade-internal closure of the Hopf gap that
`cascade-hopf-bloch-finding.md` couldn't find under Reading C
(the implicit reading of Part~II).** Under Reading G, the gauge
equivalence is $U(1)$ rather than $\mathbb{Z}_2$, and the
projective state space is automatically $\mathbb{C}P^1$, not
$\mathbb{R}P^3$.

### Cascade Born rule = QM Born rule

Under Reading G, the path-state $\tilde\psi$ is gauge-equivalent to
$R_\phi\,\tilde\psi$. Physical observables must be gauge-invariant,
so they can only depend on gauge-invariant quantities. The simplest
gauge-invariant function of two states $u, v \in S^3$ is the
*absolute value* of their complex inner product:
$$|\langle u, v\rangle_{\mathbb{C}}|^2 = (u\cdot v)^2 + (Ju\cdot v)^2.$$

This is the QM Born rule — *not* the cascade real Born rule
$(u\cdot v)^2$, which is *not* $U(1)$-invariant.

So Reading G forces the QM Born rule structurally, replacing the
cascade real Born rule with the standard QM complex Born rule.
The cascade real Born rule of Part~II §5 becomes a *gauge-fixed*
representative; the gauge-invariant rule is the QM one.

### Gauge bundle structure at distinguished layers

A path through gauge layers ($d=12, 13, 14$) has enhanced gauge
group at those layers. The cascade's matter content along such
paths transforms under the corresponding representation of the
gauge group, recovering the SM's gauge bundle structure.

### Variable Hilbert dimension

The Hilbert dim of path-state matter on a path is determined by
the gauge representation along the path:
- Singlet under all gauge groups: dim 1.
- Fundamental of $U(1)$: dim 1 (just a phase).
- Fundamental of $SU(2)$: dim 2 (qubit).
- Fundamental of $SU(3)$: dim 3 (color triplet).
- Fundamental of $SU(3) \times SU(2)$: dim 6 (e.g., quark
  doublet).

This matches the SM's representation-content at distinguished
layers. Different physical systems have different gauge
representations, hence different Hilbert dims.

## What Reading G does NOT deliver

### Hilbert dim from path length alone

The original Option 2 hope was that path length would directly give
Hilbert dim ($N$-layer path $\to \mathbb{C}^N$). Reading G says no:
Hilbert dim is determined by gauge representation, not path length.
A 213-layer cascade descent is a single gauge bundle along the
path, not a 213-dim Hilbert space.

### Composite system tensor product

For two independent path-states (e.g., two non-interacting
particles), the composite Hilbert space should be a tensor product.
Reading G doesn't directly give this; it gives a SUM of
representations (for two paths in different parts of the cascade).
Tensor product structure requires an additional cascade-internal
mechanism (e.g., paths that traverse the same cascade layers
acquire correlation via shared gauge bundle).

### Continuous-variable systems (e.g., harmonic oscillator)

Infinite-dim Hilbert spaces don't naturally come from finite gauge
representations. The cascade's harmonic-oscillator analogue would
need to be infinite-rep gauge content, which isn't present at any
finite cascade layer.

## Follow-up 1 result: Born rule from $U(1)$-gauge-invariance + Cauchy

The cascade-internal argument for the QM Born rule under Reading G
runs as follows:

1. **State**: $\psi \in \mathbb{C}^n$ with $\sum |c_i|^2 = 1$, where
   $c_i = \langle e_i, \psi\rangle$ in some measurement basis
   $\{e_i\}$.

2. **Outcome locality** (assumption): probability of outcome $i$
   depends only on $c_i$, not on other components:
   $P(e_i\mid\psi) = f(c_i)$ for some $f:\mathbb{C}\to[0,1]$.

3. **$U(1)$ gauge invariance** (cascade-internal under Reading G):
   $\psi \sim e^{i\phi}\psi$, hence $f(e^{i\phi}c) = f(c)$. Therefore
   $f(c) = g(|c|^2)$ for some $g:[0,1]\to[0,1]$.

4. **Sum-to-1 on simplex**: $\sum_i g(|c_i|^2) = 1$ on
   $\sum |c_i|^2 = 1$. Equivalently, $\sum_i g(x_i) = 1$ on the real
   simplex with $x_i = |c_i|^2$.

5. **Cauchy uniqueness for $n \geq 3$**: pick simplex points
   $(x, y, 1-x-y, 0,\ldots,0)$ and $(x+y, 1-x-y, 0,\ldots,0)$. Both
   have sum = 1, so
   $$g(x) + g(y) - g(x+y) - g(0) = 0.$$
   Setting $y = 0$ forces $g(0) = 0$. Then $g$ is Cauchy-additive,
   continuity gives $g(x) = \lambda x$, normalisation $g(1) = 1$
   gives $\lambda = 1$.
   **Therefore $g(x) = x$ uniquely, recovering the QM Born rule
   $P = |c_i|^2$.**

6. **Failure at $n = 2$**: the constraint $g(x) + g(1-x) = 1$ alone
   admits many continuous solutions. This is the same dim-$2$ gap
   Gleason's theorem encounters; closure requires compositionality
   with higher-dim systems.

The numerical verifier
`tools/verifiers/cascade_path_state_born_rule.py` confirms (5) and
(6) by exhibiting non-Born candidates that satisfy the $n=2$
constraint but fail at $n \geq 3$:

```
n = 2: g(x) = x  satisfies (Born)
       g(x) = (1 - cos(pi x))/2  satisfies (NOT Born)
       g(x) = 3x^2 - 2x^3  satisfies (NOT Born)

n = 3: only g(x) = x satisfies; non-Born candidates fail by 22-73%.

n = 4: only g(x) = x satisfies; non-Born candidates fail by 37-99%.
```

**Result**: the QM Born rule is forced cascade-internally for path
states of effective Hilbert dim $\geq 3$. The dim-$2$ gap (qubits)
is real and matches Gleason's; closure requires composition with
the rest of the cascade tower.

This is **genuine partial closure of the Hopf gap** under Option 2:
the cascade's $J$-generated $U(1)$, treated as a gauge equivalence,
forces the QM Born rule for any cascade observable that effectively
involves at least 3 dimensions. Single-qubit observables remain
under-determined by single-system Cauchy, exactly as in standard
QM.

For the cascade specifically: paths through any gauge layer
($d=12, 13, 14$) carry representations of dim $\geq 2$. Composite
systems traversing two or more gauge layers reach dim $\geq 3$
trivially, where the Born rule is forced. So the cascade's matter
content (quarks at dim 3, electroweak doublets at dim 2 ⊗ dim 2,
etc.) lives entirely in the dim-$\geq 3$ regime where Reading G's
Born rule is uniquely the QM one.
