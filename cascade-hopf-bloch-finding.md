# The Hopf bundle and the cascade's projective state space

This document attempts the lemma flagged at the close of the geometric/
topological audit (item 3.5 IGN, follow-up to
`cascade-hopf-fibration-finding.md` after the Legendre-duplication
retraction): can the cascade's $d=4$ projective state space be derived
as the Hopf base $\mathbb{C}P^1 = S^2$, with the Hopf bundle
$S^3 \to S^2$ as the cascade-internal projection?

## Result

**Partial.** The cascade has all the geometric ingredients for the Hopf
coarsening — complex structure $J$, $U(1)$ action on $S^3$ generated by
$J$, and $[L(d), R_\phi] = 0$ at every cascade step (i.e., $U(1)$ is a
genuine *dynamical symmetry* of cascade evolution). What is **not**
forced cascade-natively is the upgrade from "$U(1)$ is a dynamical
symmetry" to "$U(1)$ is a gauge equivalence on the state space." The
paper currently takes only $\mathbb{Z}_2$ as the gauge equivalence
(Part~II Remark `sp15-status`, line 478: "the cascade's state space is
$S^{d-1}/\mathbb{Z}_2$"), so the cascade's projective state space is
$\mathbb{R}P^3$ rather than $\mathbb{C}P^1$.

This is structurally analogous to the Clifford absorption status: the
ingredients are present, the closure is not forced.

## Step 1 (succeeds): $U(1)$ commutes with cascade evolution

The cascade discrete propagator at $d=4$ is (Part~II
Theorem~`discrete-schrodinger`, line 720–731):

$$
L(d) = i\,N(d), \qquad \psi_{d-1} = L(d)\,\psi_d,
$$

where the "$i$" is the operator $J:\mathbb{R}^4\to\mathbb{R}^4$ from
Part~II Theorem~`complex` (line 555–574), $J^2=-I$. The $U(1)$ action
on $S^3$ generated by $J$ is

$$
R_\phi:\psi\mapsto \cos\phi\,\psi + \sin\phi\,J\psi.
$$

Direct computation:

- $L(d)\,R_\phi\psi = J N(d)(\cos\phi\,\psi + \sin\phi\,J\psi)
   = N(d)(\cos\phi\,J\psi - \sin\phi\,\psi)$.
- $R_\phi\,L(d)\psi = R_\phi(N(d)\,J\psi)
   = N(d)(\cos\phi\,J\psi + \sin\phi\,J^2\psi)
   = N(d)(\cos\phi\,J\psi - \sin\phi\,\psi)$.

Equal. So $[L(d),\,R_\phi] = 0$ for every $\phi\in[0,2\pi)$ and every
cascade step. **$U(1)$ generated by $J$ is a dynamical symmetry of the
cascade propagator.**

## Step 2 (the gap): dynamical symmetry $\not\Rightarrow$ gauge equivalence

In standard QM, $U(1)$ global phase is BOTH a dynamical symmetry AND a
gauge equivalence of states. The latter is the move that reduces
$S^{2n+1}$ to $\mathbb{C}P^n$ via the Hopf fibration.

In the cascade as currently written, only $\mathbb{Z}_2$ antipode is
treated as a gauge equivalence (Part~II line 478). $U(1)$ is a
dynamical symmetry (Step 1) but not declared a gauge.

**This is consistent but not standard QM.** The cascade's stated state
space is $\mathbb{R}P^3$ ($S^3$ modulo antipode), three-dimensional;
standard QM's Bloch sphere is $\mathbb{C}P^1 = S^2$, two-dimensional.
The cascade has one extra continuous degree of freedom relative to QM —
exactly the $U(1)/\mathbb{Z}_2 \cong S^1$ Hopf fibre.

## Step 3 (the candidates that fail): cascade-internal closures

Three plausible-looking closures, each insufficient as currently
available:

### 3.1 Step-unresolvability

Part~II line 715–718 states: "The Schrödinger equation is what the 4D
observer reconstructs as an effective description **when individual
cascade steps are not resolved**." If single steps are unresolvable,
then states differing by one step's phase ($\psi$ vs $i\psi$) are
indistinguishable to the observer.

**This gives at most $\mathbb{Z}_4$ coarsening, not $U(1)$.** The
single-step phase is multiplication by $i$, generating a $\mathbb{Z}_4$
subgroup $\{1, i, -1, -i\}$ of $U(1)$. The orbit of $\psi$ under
arbitrary integer numbers of cascade steps stays in $\mathbb{Z}_4$;
the steps don't densify in $U(1)$.

So $S^3/\mathbb{Z}_4$ (a lens space $L(4,1)$, three-dimensional) is
the strongest coarsening step-unresolvability gives. Still not
$\mathbb{C}P^1$.

### 3.2 Continuous-time Schrödinger evolution

The continuum-limit Schrödinger equation
$i\,\partial_t\psi = \mathcal{H}(d)\psi$ (Part~II
Corollary~`schrodinger`, line 766–802) generates continuous $U(1)$
orbits when $\mathcal{H}$ is a scalar (which $\mathcal{H}(d)$ is —
it's the function $(1-N(d))/N(d)^2$). So the dynamical orbit of any
state under time evolution does cover $U(1)$.

**But dynamical evolution $\neq$ gauge equivalence.** States at
different times $t$ are physically distinct in QM — they have
different correlations with later measurements. Identifying
$\psi(t_1) \sim \psi(t_2)$ for arbitrary $t_1, t_2$ would collapse all
times to one and trivialise dynamics. So the dynamical $U(1)$ orbit
isn't a gauge.

### 3.3 J-invariance of observables (the closest candidate)

If cascade physical observables are restricted to $J$-invariant
Hermitian operators on $\mathbb{R}^4$ (equivalently: Hermitian
operators on $\mathbb{C}^2$), then:

- Their eigenspaces come in $J$-pairs (each complex eigenvector is a
  $J$-invariant 2-real-dim subspace of $\mathbb{R}^4$).
- The eigenspace through $\psi$ is the Hopf fibre
  $\{e^{i\phi}\psi:\phi\in[0,2\pi)\}$.
- Measurement outcomes are points in $\mathbb{C}P^1$ (one Hopf fibre
  per outcome).
- The cascade real Born rule pair-summed over a Hopf fibre equals the
  QM complex Born rule:
  $$
  (e_i\cdot u)^2 + (Je_i\cdot u)^2 = |\langle e_i,u\rangle_{\mathbb{C}}|^2.
  $$

**This works arithmetically.** The standard linear-algebra identity
$|\langle u,v\rangle_{\mathbb{C}}|^2 = (\mathrm{Re}\langle u,v\rangle)^2
+ (\mathrm{Im}\langle u,v\rangle)^2 = (u\cdot v)^2 + (Ju\cdot v)^2$
(using $\mathrm{Im}\langle u,v\rangle = (Ju)\cdot v$) shows that
pair-summing the cascade real Born rule over a $J$-orbit recovers QM
exactly.

**What's missing:** a cascade-native reason that physical observables
*must* be $J$-invariant. Two attempts that don't quite work:

- "Observables must commute with dynamics for well-defined values."
  False in standard QM — only conserved quantities commute with $H$;
  non-conserved observables (position, e.g.) are valid.

- "Hermitian-on-$\mathbb{C}$ is the natural choice given complex
  structure." Plausible but not forced — symmetric-on-$\mathbb{R}$ is
  also a natural choice given the underlying real geometry.

The cascade's Cauchy-additivity argument (Part~II Step~4 of
Theorem~`born`, line 401–446) derives the Born rule on the *real*
simplex $\Delta^{d-1}$, requiring $d\geq 3$. Re-running the argument
on a *complex* simplex would give the QM Born rule with $J$-paired
basis vectors, but requires choosing complex over real bases as the
measurement objects.

## Step 4: what would close the gap

A cascade-native closure of the Hopf-bundle structure would derive
that physical observables are $J$-paired (Hermitian on
$\mathbb{C}^2$). Candidates worth pursuing:

1. **Frame-function uniqueness on the complex simplex.** Re-run the
   Part~II Cauchy-additivity argument on the simplex over a complex
   basis ($n$-vectors in $\mathbb{C}^n$ rather than $2n$-vectors in
   $\mathbb{R}^{2n}$). If the frame-function constraint
   $\sum_i g(|c_i|^2) = 1$ over complex bases has the same Cauchy
   solution $g(x)=x$, the QM Born rule follows. The question is
   whether the cascade has a structural reason to use complex bases
   over real bases.

2. **Real-Born-rule inconsistency in dim $\geq 3$.** Gleason's
   original theorem gives the Born rule on Hilbert dimension $\geq 3$,
   but only over complex bases. The real analogue may admit additional
   solutions inconsistent with the cascade's slicing measure (analogous
   to how Gleason fails at $d=2$). If the real Born rule admits
   inconsistencies that the cascade cannot tolerate, the system is
   forced to the complex Born rule, hence to Hopf coarsening.

3. **Compactification-radius forcing.** Part~II Theorem~`reff`
   (line 875–899) gives effective radius
   $R_{\rm eff}(d) = 1/\sqrt{d+3}$ at each cascade step. The
   integrated-out direction has finite extent, which may correspond
   to the Hopf fibre direction in some cascade-native sense. This
   is speculative; would need explicit identification of the
   compactified direction with the Hopf fibre at $d=4$.

## Comparison to the Legendre-duplication retraction

The previous attempt (`cascade-hopf-fibration-finding.md`) claimed an
exact identity tying cascade quantities to Hopf fibrations. That claim
was retracted: the identity is Legendre duplication in disguise, holds
at every $n$, and has no Hopf content.

This attempt is different in kind:

- The previous claim was an arithmetic identity that turned out empty.
- This claim is a structural-geometric question: does the cascade's
  projective state space coincide with the Hopf base?

The honest answer to the latter is "with one explicit additional
hypothesis ($J$-invariance of observables), yes; without it, no." The
gap is real and well-defined, and the closure candidates in Step 4
are concrete research questions, not arithmetic verifications.

## Recommendation

Do **not** add a Hopf-bundle remark to Part~II claiming the cascade's
projective space is $\mathbb{C}P^1$. The current paper takes
$\mathbb{R}P^3$, and that's consistent with the cascade's
real-Born-rule derivation.

Do flag in Part~II the **gap between the dynamical $U(1)$ symmetry
(Step 1 above) and the stated $\mathbb{Z}_2$ gauge equivalence**.
This is internal to the paper and does not require new mathematics.
A reader would naturally ask why $U(1)$ is not the gauge; the paper
should answer or acknowledge that it doesn't.

The Hopf-bundle question is then framed as: *if* the cascade's gauge
is upgraded from $\mathbb{Z}_2$ to $U(1)$, then the projective space
becomes $\mathbb{C}P^1$ via the Hopf bundle, and the cascade Born
rule becomes the QM Born rule. Whether the upgrade can be forced
cascade-natively is open.

This is more substantive than the Legendre-duplication identity but
less than a full derivation. Per austerity (Prelude Principle 2.2):
the gap should be flagged, not silently glossed.
